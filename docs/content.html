Content & voice

Because the system is grayscale, the words do most of the work — they're the first thing a client reacts to. The voice is plain, direct, and reassuring, and it's written to be read by someone who is stressed, older, or reading in a second language.

Voice & tone

The voice is constant; the tone flexes with the moment. We sound like a calm, competent person — never a brochure, never a robot.

Plain
Common words, short sentences. If a simpler word works, use it.
Direct
Say what to do and what happens next. Lead with the point.
Reassuring
On anxious moments — results, costs, errors — be calm and specific, not breezy.
Human
Address the reader as "you." Use contractions. Skip jargon and hype.

Plain language

Patient-facing copy targets a 6th–8th-grade reading level. That isn't dumbing down — it's the level at which a worried reader actually absorbs instructions.

  • Short sentences, one idea each. Prefer the active voice ("Bring your insurance card," not "Insurance cards should be brought").
  • Common words over clinical ones; when a medical term is unavoidable, define it on first use.
  • Front-load the point — the reader may not get past the first line.
  • Break dense instructions into a numbered list or steps rather than a paragraph.
Plainer is clearer

Avoid · "Patients are required to maintain a fasting state for a minimum of eight hours prior to their scheduled appointment."

Prefer · "Don't eat or drink anything for 8 hours before your appointment."

Mechanics

The small, consistent choices that keep copy looking deliberate.

ElementRuleExample
CapitalizationSentence case for headings, buttons, and labels."Find a doctor", not "Find A Doctor".
NumbersFigures for anything scannable — data, steps, times, ages."8 hours", "3 locations", "ages 5–12".
Dates & timesSpell the month; use a clear time format."March 4, 8:30 a.m."
TerminologyPick one term per concept and keep it. Define jargon once."visit" everywhere, not "visit / appointment / encounter".
PunctuationNo exclamation points in UI copy. One idea per sentence.—

UI copy

The words inside components carry the most weight per character. Keep them specific.

Links
Describe the destination. "See visiting hours", never "click here" or "read more".
Buttons
Start with a verb and name the outcome. "Book a visit", "Save changes".
Labels
Name the field plainly; put format help in hint text, not the label.
Errors
Say what happened and how to fix it, calmly. "Enter a date in the future," not "Invalid input".
Empty states
Explain why it's empty and the one next step. Never a dead end.
Helper text
Short, before the moment of need — under the field, not in a tooltip.

Writing for the audience

Healthcare and higher-ed readers skew older and span every literacy and language level. A few habits matter more here than elsewhere.

  • Older readers: larger default text, generous spacing, and no idioms or wordplay that assume cultural context.
  • Limited-English readers: plain words, literal phrasing, and content that survives machine translation. Where the system offers language matching (e.g. provider languages), name it plainly.
  • Stressed readers: on results, billing, and instructions, be concrete and lead with what to do.

Reading-level and health-literacy targets are documented in the Resources Evidence model.
