Heroes

The first block on a page. Five purpose-built variants: editorial for big-type moments, split for image + text, centered for landing pages, stat-led when numbers carry the message, and inverse for any of the above flipped to dark.

Anatomy

A hero stacks an eyebrow, a title, an optional lead, and an actions row. Variants change the layout of those parts; the parts themselves stay constant.

Default (editorial)
Patient story

After her stroke, Maya walked again. Now she's running marathons.

Twelve months of intensive therapy at the Riverside — and a team that wouldn't quit.

Read Maya's story Watch the film

When to use

  • Use editorial for a single emphatic landing statement; italics inside the title turn serif for an editorial moment.
  • Use split when an image is part of the message — patient photo, lab shot, product image.
  • Use centered for short-form landing pages, campaigns, or single-purpose marketing pages.
  • Use stat-led when three numbers carry more weight than a paragraph (annual report, research overview).
  • Use inverse sparingly — once per page at most — for high-emphasis CTAs or section anchors.

When not to use

  • Do not stack heroes; the hero is the first block, not a recurring section header.
  • Do not use a hero on detail pages where a tighter .wire-h1 would do.
  • Do not crop split-variant images to portrait — the layout assumes 16:9 / 4:3 / square.

Accessibility

  • The hero title must be the page h1; do not use the hero as a section header below another h1.
  • Decorative italic in the editorial variant is purely typographic — do not put meaning in the italic alone.
  • Split variant places media after text in DOM so screen readers hear the message first.
  • Inverse variant maintains 4.5:1 contrast for lead text via the muted gray on dark background; secondary buttons re-style for the inverse surface.
