Typography

A single scale that carries everything from a 12px eyebrow to an 88px display headline. Hierarchy comes from size, weight, and space — never color.

Anatomy

A typographic block typically stacks an eyebrow, a heading, a lead paragraph, and body copy. The eyebrow names the context, the heading carries the idea, the lead carries the second sentence, body fills in.

Default stack

Patient programs

Therapy that starts the day you arrive.

Most rehabilitation hospitals wait until you are stable. We start the moment it is safe — because the brain rewires fastest in the first weeks after injury.

Body text is the workhorse: set at 16px, comfortable line height, capped at roughly seventy characters per line. Long-form prose belongs inside .wire-prose to get the right rhythm between paragraphs and headings.

Caption · supporting context, image credits, helper text.

When to use

  • Use .wire-display for one moment per page — a hero or landing statement.
  • Use .wire-h1–.wire-h6 in document order; do not skip levels for visual size.
  • Use .wire-lead directly after a heading to introduce a section.
  • Use .wire-eyebrow to label a section's category in two or three words.

When not to use

  • Do not use .wire-display inside cards or compact blocks — it will fight the surrounding scale.
  • Do not restyle a heading to look like another; pick the right class instead.
  • Do not use .wire-caption as body copy — it is intentionally muted and small.

Variants

Every heading class has matching h1–h6 element styling. The display class adds an optional serif italic for editorial moments.

Prose container / .wire-prose

A confident outline of the section

Inside a .wire-prose wrapper, paragraphs and headings get the right rhythm automatically. Use this for long-form editorial, articles, and policy pages.

Subsequent paragraphs gain a comfortable top margin, and headings that follow paragraphs get a larger break above them so sections breathe.

A subhead inside prose

Headings inside prose pull their following content closer, so the relationship between heading and body reads as a unit.

Accessibility

  • Headings ship as both element styles (h1–h6) and utility classes (.wire-h1–.wire-h6) so semantics never have to lose to visuals.
  • Use document order: one h1 per page, then nested levels in sequence. Skipping levels breaks assistive-tech outlines.
  • Body text exceeds 4.5:1 contrast against the page background; muted text exceeds 4.5:1 against white.
  • Paragraphs cap at roughly seventy characters per line by default, which keeps comfortable reading without manual width constraints.
