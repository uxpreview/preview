Elevation & shadow

Shadow signals that something floats above the page — a menu, a dialog, a toast. It is used sparingly: in a grayscale system, a hairline border does most of the work of separating things, and shadow is reserved for genuine elevation.

Tokens

Four shadow tokens, each a soft, low-opacity cast. They get larger and more diffuse as the element rises, the way a real object's shadow does.

The layering model

Think in three planes. Most of the interface lives on the base; shadow only appears as an element leaves it.

  • Base (0) — the page and everything in normal flow. No shadow; borders separate regions.
  • Raised (xs–sm) — a surface lifted just off the page: a card you can interact with, a sticky bar.
  • Overlay (md–lg) — content stacked above everything, with a backdrop or clear separation: menus and popovers (md), modals, drawers, and toasts (lg).

When to use each

LevelPlaneWhere
xsRaisedSubtle lift — a resting card, an input on focus.
smRaisedSticky header or bar that scrolls over content.
mdOverlayMenus, popovers, tooltips — small floating layers.
lgOverlayModal, drawer, toast — the top of the stack.

Shadow vs. borders

The single most useful rule here: borders separate, shadows elevate. Two things sitting on the same plane are divided by a hairline border, not a shadow. Reach for shadow only when an element has genuinely lifted off the page and needs to read as floating above its neighbors.

Do
Use a border to delineate a card, panel, or region that sits in normal flow.
Do
Use shadow for layers that overlap other content — menus, dialogs, toasts.
Don't
Add a shadow to a flat card just for emphasis; let weight, space, and borders carry hierarchy.
Don't
Stack heavy shadows; one level per element, matched to its plane.
