Baclofen BAK-loh-fen

A muscle relaxant used to treat spasticity caused by spinal cord injury, multiple sclerosis, cerebral palsy, and stroke. Available as tablets, a liquid, or as a continuous-infusion pump (intrathecal baclofen).

What it's for

Baclofen treats spasticity — the tightness, stiffness, and involuntary spasms in muscles that can happen when nerve signals from the brain to the muscles are disrupted. The most common reasons your doctor might prescribe it:

  • Spinal cord injury, especially incomplete injuries with preserved sensation below the level of injury.
  • Multiple sclerosis with significant spasticity affecting walking, transfers, or comfort.
  • Cerebral palsy in children and adults.
  • Spasticity following stroke or traumatic brain injury.

It doesn't treat the underlying cause of spasticity. It reduces the symptom — making movement easier, sleep more restful, and positioning more comfortable.

How to take it

Oral baclofen (tablets or liquid)

  • Take with or without food — food doesn't change how it works, but taking it with a small snack can ease stomach upset.
  • Swallow tablets whole with water. Don't crush or chew unless your doctor or pharmacist tells you to.
  • Doses are usually divided three times a day. Your doctor will start low and increase the dose gradually over weeks until your spasticity is controlled.
  • Don't double up if you miss a dose — see missed or extra dose below.

Intrathecal baclofen (pump)

  • The pump delivers a small, steady dose directly into the fluid around your spinal cord. You'll have it refilled at the clinic every 1–6 months.
  • Never miss a refill appointment. Withdrawal from an empty pump is a medical emergency.
  • Your pump has a programmer card. Carry it with you, and tell airport security and any other doctor you see that you have a pump.

Side effects

Most people who take baclofen have at least one side effect, especially during the first weeks and after a dose increase. Most are mild and improve with time.

Interactions

Tell your doctor and pharmacist about every medicine, supplement, and recreational substance you take — even ones that seem unrelated.

  • Alcohol compounds the drowsiness and confusion. Avoid or sharply limit alcohol while on baclofen.
  • Opioid pain medicines (oxycodone, hydrocodone, fentanyl), benzodiazepines (alprazolam, clonazepam, diazepam), and sleep medicines (zolpidem) all add to drowsiness and can affect breathing. Use only with close supervision.
  • Tricyclic antidepressants can increase the weakness baclofen causes.
  • Blood-pressure medicines — baclofen can lower blood pressure further. Your doctor may adjust doses.
  • Drugs that affect the kidneys — baclofen is cleared by the kidneys; doses may need to be lower if kidney function is reduced.

When to call your doctor

Right away

Call your doctor today if you have any of these:

  • New or worsening confusion, hallucinations, or mood changes
  • A fall or near-fall related to weakness or drowsiness
  • New trouble urinating, or pain when you do
  • Side effects that are interfering with your day
  • You're thinking about stopping the medicine
Emergency

Call 911 for any of these:

  • Seizure
  • Hallucinations and high fever
  • Severe trouble breathing
  • Symptoms of pump malfunction (sudden return of spasticity, high fever, itching, low blood pressure)

Missed or extra dose

Missed a dose? Take it as soon as you remember, unless it's almost time for your next dose. If it is, skip the missed dose. Never take two doses at once.

Took an extra dose by accident? Call Poison Control at 1-800-222-1222 (US). Watch for unusual drowsiness, weakness, or trouble breathing — get emergency help if any of those appear.

Storage

  • Store tablets at room temperature, away from heat and moisture. Keep the bottle closed.
  • Oral solution may need refrigeration — check the label.
  • Keep out of reach of children and pets.
  • Don't keep expired medicine. Take it to a pharmacy or community drug take-back program — don't flush.

Similar medications

Other medicines that treat spasticity. Your doctor will pick based on which side effects you tolerate, what other medicines you take, and how well each works for you.

  • Tizanidine (Zanaflex) Alpha-2 agonist · shorter-acting · drops blood pressure
  • Dantrolene (Dantrium) Acts on muscle directly · requires liver monitoring
  • Diazepam (Valium) Benzodiazepine · more sedating · habit-forming
  • Botulinum toxin (Botox) injections Targets specific muscles · injected every 3 months
