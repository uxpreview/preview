Berg Balance Scale BBS

A 14-item performance-based measure of static and dynamic balance, scored on a five-point ordinal scale (0–4) for a maximum of 56 points. Widely used in clinical and research settings since 1989.

Overview

The Berg Balance Scale (BBS) was developed by Katherine Berg and colleagues in 1989 to evaluate balance among older adults and has since become one of the most widely cited balance measures in the rehabilitation literature. The scale comprises 14 functional tasks of increasing difficulty — from sitting unsupported to standing on one leg — each scored on a five-point ordinal scale from 0 (cannot perform) to 4 (performs independently with control).

Total scores range from 0 to 56, with higher scores indicating better balance. The measure is performance-based: the clinician observes the patient executing each task and assigns a score based on prescribed criteria. It is not a self-report instrument, and reliability depends on the assessor following the published administration protocol.

Clinical note. The BBS does not assess dynamic balance during gait. Pair with a gait-focused measure (Dynamic Gait Index, Functional Gait Assessment) for fall-risk screening that covers walking conditions.

Area of assessment

Primary construct
Functional balance — both static (sitting, standing) and dynamic (transfers, turning, reaching).
Body part
Whole body (lower extremity, trunk, postural control).
ICF domain
Activity. Some items also touch on body functions (neuromuscular and movement-related functions, b730–b789).
Assessment type
Performance-based, observational, clinician-administered.
Constructs not assessed
Balance during gait at speed, reactive postural responses, sensory integration, fall history, fear of falling.

Populations & conditions

The BBS has been validated in dozens of patient populations. Strength of evidence and recommended use vary by condition.

Recommended populations and evidence level for the Berg Balance Scale.
Condition Recommendation Evidence level Notes
Stroke Highly recommended 1a · meta-analysis Ceiling effect in high-functioning ambulators; consider Mini-BESTest after week 12 post-stroke.
Parkinson disease Highly recommended 1b · systematic review Sensitive to change in early-to-moderate disease (H&Y 1–3).
Traumatic brain injury Recommended 2a · controlled trial Use alongside a cognitive screen; impaired insight can affect performance.
Older adults (community-dwelling) Highly recommended 1a · meta-analysis Strong evidence for fall-risk prediction at <45.
Older adults (residential) Recommended 2a · cohort study Original validation population; remains the reference standard.
Multiple sclerosis Recommended 2b · observational Consider Mini-BESTest for higher-functioning ambulators (EDSS ≤ 4).
Vestibular disorders Use with caution 3 · expert opinion Insensitive to vestibular-specific dysfunction; pair with Functional Gait Assessment.
Lower-limb amputation Use with caution 3 · expert opinion Items 13–14 (tandem stance, single-leg stance) often inapplicable.

Cut-off & interpretation

The most commonly cited interpretive bands come from the original community-dwelling older-adult validation (Berg et al., 1992) and have been refined for clinical populations in subsequent work.

Score bands and clinical interpretation.
Score range Interpretation Clinical implication
45–56 Independent Low fall risk; functional ambulation likely.
41–44 Functional balance with limitations Walking with supervision recommended.
21–40 Acceptable balance with assistance Assistive device often required; moderate fall risk.
0–20 Impaired balance Wheelchair-bound or requires significant assistance for transfers.

Population-specific cutoffs. Stroke survivors: a score < 45 has been associated with increased fall risk (Stevenson 2001). Parkinson disease: a score < 47 differentiates fallers from non-fallers with 76% sensitivity (Landers 2008).

Administration

Setup

Set up a quiet space approximately 8 ft × 8 ft with a clear floor. You will need:

  • One chair with armrests (for transfers)
  • One chair without armrests (for sitting items)
  • A yardstick or measuring tape (for the functional reach item)
  • A standard step or stool, approximately 8 inches high
  • A stopwatch
  • The 14-item scoring sheet

Procedure

  1. Explain each task before scoring and demonstrate when needed.
  2. Allow the patient to choose the easier option if asked (e.g., which leg to stand on).
  3. Score the worst-case attempt; if the patient performs the task more than once, score the lowest performance.
  4. Items 13 (tandem stance) and 14 (single-leg stance) are timed — record the longest of three attempts.
  5. Record time and the total score on the scoring sheet.

The 14 items

  1. Sitting to standing
  2. Standing unsupported
  3. Sitting unsupported, feet on floor
  4. Standing to sitting
  5. Transfers
  6. Standing unsupported with eyes closed
  7. Standing unsupported with feet together
  8. Reaching forward with outstretched arm
  9. Pick up object from floor
  10. Turning to look behind
  11. Turning 360 degrees
  12. Placing alternate foot on stool
  13. Standing with one foot in front (tandem)
  14. Standing on one leg

Clinical considerations

Strengths

  • Free, brief, requires no specialized training or equipment beyond what is already in most clinics.
  • Strong evidence base across many populations; widely understood by interdisciplinary teams.
  • Total score is easy to communicate to patients and families.

Limitations

  • Ceiling effect. Higher-functioning ambulators frequently score the maximum, limiting sensitivity to change in later rehab phases.
  • No gait component. Does not assess balance during walking; pair with a gait measure for comprehensive fall-risk screening.
  • Reactive balance not assessed. The BBS measures volitional balance only — not the postural responses triggered by an unexpected perturbation.
  • Ordinal scoring. Total scores should be interpreted within bands; small numeric differences may not represent clinically meaningful change unless they exceed the MDC95.
