Patient story · Stroke recovery

After his stroke, Marcus walked his daughter down the aisle.

Eight months ago, the left side of his body wouldn't move. Last Saturday, he danced.

By Elena Vasquez · May 15, 2026 · 8 minute read

Marcus Reyes was driving his daughter to a dress fitting when the world tilted. He pulled over on the shoulder of I-94 and called 911 with the hand that still worked. Twenty-eight minutes later, he was at Northwestern Memorial. A clot in his middle cerebral artery had cut off blood to the left side of his brain. He was 57.

The neurologists got the clot out within the window. The damage was real but contained. By the next morning, Marcus could speak. He couldn't move his right leg. His right arm hung as if it belonged to someone else.

"You're going to AbilityLab," his wife Diana told him three days in. She said it like a fact. Two weeks later, an ambulance brought him through the Erie Street entrance.

"The first month, I just wanted to walk to the window"

Marcus was admitted to the stroke service on the seventh floor. His care team — a physiatrist, two physical therapists, an occupational therapist, a speech-language pathologist, and a rehab nurse named Kenya who he says "saved my marriage by yelling at me to do the homework" — met him on day one.

The first goal, Diana remembers, was modest. "He wanted to walk to the window. That was it. Twelve feet." It took him six days.

Dr. James Chen Marcus's attending physiatrist

Dr. James Chen, his attending physiatrist, says the trajectory wasn't unusual. "Recovery isn't a straight line. It's a thousand small wins, and your job is to notice them." What was unusual, Chen says, was Marcus's stubborn arithmetic — he kept a notebook with numbers. Steps walked. Stairs climbed. Buttons buttoned. Seconds of grip on the dynamometer.

"By month two, the numbers stopped going up every day," Chen says. "That's the part most patients hate. They think they've plateaued. They haven't — they're consolidating. Marcus understood that."

A daughter, a deadline, a dress

Lucia had set the wedding for May 9. She didn't move it when her father had a stroke. "If she'd moved it, he would have been crushed," Diana says. "She knew that. We all knew that."

The wedding became the goal that organized everything. Walking down the aisle meant 47 feet, on a slight uphill, on a runner that bunched under a cane. The team broke it into pieces. Endurance. Balance on uneven surfaces. Holding eye contact without losing his footing. The first father-daughter dance the bride had agreed to was 90 seconds long.

Marcus walked Lucia down the aisle without a cane. He says he was holding her arm partly because that's what you do at a wedding, and partly because he was terrified of his right knee. Halfway down, she whispered, "You got this, Dad," and he says he almost cried then and decided to wait until later.

He danced. Ninety seconds, then the song faded, and then they played another one and he danced for that one too. Diana filmed it. Kenya was there, in the back, with two other therapists from the seventh floor.

After the wedding

Marcus still has outpatient therapy three times a week. His right hand fatigues. He gets tired in the late afternoon and his speech slurs when he's exhausted. He doesn't drive yet — Diana drives, or he takes the Red Line, and he says that's fine.

"He talks about it like it's a job," Chen says. "He's clocking in. That's the thing about recovery. It doesn't end. It just gets quieter."

My daughter never asked me to walk her down the aisle. She just assumed I would. That assumption is what I was working for.

Marcus Reyes

Asked what he'd tell another patient at the start of their rehab, Marcus thinks for a long time. "Pick a thing," he finally says. "A real thing. Not 'walk again' — too big. A wedding. A trip. A grandkid's birthday. Then count backwards from it. The therapists are good at that math."
