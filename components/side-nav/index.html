Local navigation

Stable

The family of within-section navigation: left rails, a horizontal section bar, a contextual right rail, master-detail, and the mobile drawer. This page is the decision guide — which local nav to use, why, and where — plus the deep-hierarchy model for pages that sit many levels down.

Choosing a variant

Two questions decide it. First, is navigation or content the protagonist? Nav-primary screens (consoles, dashboards) lean on a persistent left rail; content-primary screens (articles, profiles) keep content full-width and push contextual nav to a horizontal bar or the right rail. Second, how deep is the IA? Shallow and few peers favor a horizontal bar; broad or growing favors a left rail; truly deep is solved by scoping the rail and letting the breadcrumb carry ancestry (see below). Local nav should always read as less salient than the global nav.

If…Use
≤1 level, 2–7 peers, content-firstHorizontal section sub-nav
Broad/growing IA, 4–12 peers, nav-firstLeft rail (wire-sidenav)
Long reading pageQuiet left rail + right-rail in-page nav
Deepest node is an entity viewed many waysMaster-detail (list + tabs)
Small screenOff-canvas drawer holding the rail
≥4 levels deepScope the rail + breadcrumb for ancestry

Three patterns

One component answers three section questions. Pick the lightest pattern that fits: local nav for peers, secondary nav for children, the section tree only when the IA is wide enough that users lose their place.

Local nav — peer pages

A flat list of siblings within the current section, titled by their parent. Use it when the page sits among 4–12 peers a user is likely to compare.

The five variants

One family, five form factors. The left rail and the section bar are components; the right rail, master-detail, and mobile drawer are compositions of parts you already have.

1 · Left rail — within-section

The workhorse for within-section movement — peers, parent + children, or a shallow tree (all three demoed under Three patterns above). Reach for it on nav-primary screens and growing IAs; it sits on the leading (left) edge. Add wire-sidenav--sticky to pin it beside long content.

2 · Horizontal section sub-nav

A single row of peers under the masthead — the inverted-L. Keeps the content column full-width. Best for 2–7 peers, one level.

5 · Mobile drawer

On small screens the rail collapses into an off-canvas drawer opened by the top-nav burger (data-wire-drawer-open); the same wire-sidenav markup rides inside it. No drilldown — depth is handled by scoping + breadcrumb.

Deep hierarchy & the dead-end leaf

A page five levels down with no children is not a taller rail problem — it's a division of labor. Don't render five indented levels. Scope the rail to the nearest branch and let the breadcrumb carry the way back.

Ancestors (going back)Breadcrumb — scales to any depth
Siblings + currentRail scoped to the nearest branch (__link--parent + sibling list)
ChildrenNone — it's a leaf; the rail shows lateral peers instead

This keeps best practice and the system's two-level rail cap aligned: depth lives in the breadcrumb, not in indentation.

When to use

  • Use a side nav for movement within a section — peers, children, or a shallow tree.
  • Cap nesting at two levels (parent → siblings → children of current). Deeper structures belong in multi-page IA, not a denser rail.
  • For positions inside a single long page, use In-page nav; for the site's top-level destinations, the Top nav.
  • Above ~15 peers a rail becomes a scroll-jail — switch to a search-driven listing page.

Do / Don't

Do

  • Label each rail for the section it serves.
  • Set aria-current="page" on the active link, alongside .is-current.
  • Pick the lightest of the three patterns that answers the user's question.

Don't

  • Nest more than two levels deep.
  • Use a rail for a section with only one page.
  • Rely on the accent bar alone — keep aria-current for assistive tech.

Structure

  • The rail is a vertical flex column of groups; groups after the first add top margin for separation.
  • Links are full-width blocks with a transparent leading border that turns to --color-accent on the current item — the marker reads even in grayscale.
  • A __link--parent renders the section title as a heavier link with an underline rule; a __sublist indents children behind a thin guide line.
  • The --sticky modifier pins the rail within a scrolling content column on tablet+, releasing below 48em.

Landmark & labeling

  • Wrap each rail in <nav> with a label describing the section ("Conditions we treat", "Research sections").
  • A page may carry several nav landmarks (rail, breadcrumb, in-page, section bar). Give each a distinct aria-label so they're tellable apart.

Current page

  • Set aria-current="page" on the active link. The .is-current class is the visual marker; the ARIA attribute is independent and required for assistive tech.
  • The accent bar and weight change aren't enough on their own — keep the attribute even though the styling is obvious.

Targets & sticky behavior

  • Top-level links meet the WCAG 2.5.8 target size; sub-list links are smaller, so verify ≥24px on any new nesting.
  • A plain rail isn't sticky by default. If you wrap one in a sticky container, drop the sticky below 48em (the reflow guard the in-page TOC uses) so it never traps content on small screens.
