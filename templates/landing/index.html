Landing page

Landing shell

A marketing or section homepage: it opens with a hero, then stacks alternating content bands — features, a card grid, a bold inverse moment, resources — and closes on a contact prompt and the footer. The recipe one level above components; it composes them into a whole page.

  • Based on the landing page shell
  • Example: Riverside Medical Center

A real page built only from the system — the Riverside Medical Center homepage. Scroll it inside the frame, or open it full-width to use it at real scale.

What it is

A landing page is the front door to an organization or a section of it. It does one job well: orient a first-time visitor and send them to the few things they came for. It leads with a hero that states who you are and the primary action, then uses a vertical stack of content bands — alternating surfaces to mark the rhythm — to surface services, proof, and resources without burying them in navigation.

When to use

  • An organization homepage, or the home of a major section (a service line, a campaign).
  • A page whose job is orientation and routing, not a single transaction or a long read.
  • When you have a few distinct things to promote and want them scannable in one scroll.

When not to use

  • For a single task or transaction — use a focused flow, and end it on a confirmation, not a landing page.
  • For a long-form read — that's the article shell.
  • For a searchable set of results — that's the listing shell (a directory).

Regions, top to bottom

The landing shell is a fixed spine of regions. Each is built from components that already carry their own docs — the template is the order and the choices, not new CSS.

RegionBuilt fromPurpose
Global headerTop nav · Mega menu · DrawerIdentity and primary navigation; the drawer carries it on mobile.
HeroHero (editorial)Who you are and the one action that matters most.
Intro bandTwo-column · ButtonA short positioning statement and the next-most-likely route.
Featured gridCard gridServices, programs, or news as equal-weight, scannable teasers.
HighlightTwo-column · MediaOne program or story given room, media beside copy.
Inverse momentu-bg-inverse · Quote / statA bold dark band for proof — a testimonial or a few numbers.
ResourcesArticle list · Linked listDeeper content for visitors who want to read on.
Contact / CTACallout · FieldA clear prompt to get in touch or take the primary action.
FooterFooter (inverse)The full site map, contact, and legal.

Content model

What the page needs from content before it can be filled in:

  • Hero — a headline (the org's promise in a sentence), a one-line sub, one primary action, optional secondary.
  • Featured grid — 3–6 items of one kind (don't mix services with news), each a title + one-line description + link.
  • Highlight — one story: an eyebrow, a headline, a short paragraph, a media slot, a link.
  • Proof — either one quote with attribution, or 3–4 stats (a number + a label each).
  • Resources — a short list of links with dates or read-times.
  • Contact — a single prompt with one field or one button; don't open a full form on the homepage.

Composition notes

  • Alternate surfaces, sparingly — default and u-bg-subtle bands set the rhythm; reserve one u-bg-inverse band for a single high-emphasis moment, not several.
  • One primary action — the hero owns it; later CTAs are secondary so the page has a clear top priority.
  • One card type per grid — a grid of person cards, or of content cards, never a mix; the silhouette break is jarring.
  • Edit down — every band competes for the same scroll; cut a band before crowding it.

Landmarks & structure

A whole-page contract: the parts each carry their own ARIA, but the page is responsible for putting them in the right order and not repeating roles.

  • One <header> banner, one <main>, one <footer> contentinfo; every <nav> labelled (Primary, Breadcrumb, In-page).
  • A skip link as the first focusable element, jumping to #main.
  • Content bands are <section>s; give each a heading so it shows in the document outline.

Heading order

  • Exactly one h1 — the hero headline. The org name in the header is not the h1.
  • Each band's title is an h2; subsections step to h3 without skipping a level.
  • Card titles take the level that fits their band (usually h3), not a fixed level.

Keyboard & focus

  • Focus order follows source order top to bottom; no positive tabindex.
  • The mobile menu is a drawer — focus trapped while open, returned to the trigger on close.
  • The mega menu opens on click and closes on Escape, moving focus into the panel.
  • Every interactive element shows the shared 2px focus ring; targets clear the 24px floor.

Audience affordances

  • For healthcare and older audiences, keep the text-size control and the help bar / phone affordance reachable — a landing page is often the first and most-shared page.
  • Respect prefers-reduced-motion; any hero or reveal motion is decorative and drops out.
  • Grayscale throughout — meaning never rides on color; the inverse band earns emphasis from contrast and weight.
